Historical Background of Andaman

The history of the Andaman and Nicobar Islands stretches back to the Ramayana period, where it was known as Handuman. Over time, the name evolved. By the 1st Century AD, Ptolemy referred to the region as Agadaemon Angademan. Throughout history, these islands have attracted visitors from various corners of the globe, including Arab travelers in the 9th Century, Marco Polo in the 13th Century (who described it as Angamanian), Friar Odoric in the 14th Century, and Caesar Fredericke in the 16th Century.

A significant turning point occurred in the 17th century when Lieutenant Archibald Blair of the Royal Indian Navy established a naval base on a small island near South Andaman. This marked the initial steps towards civilization on the islands, as forests were cleared, cottages were built, and kitchen gardens and orchards were cultivated.

The first formal colony was established on Chatham Island, encompassing 12 acres. In 1857, "The Andaman Committee," led by Dr. Frederic John Mouat, was formed to identify the optimal location for a penal settlement. On January 22, 1858 (for the second time), Captain H Man raised the Union Jack at Port Blair, officially establishing and managing the Penal System.
